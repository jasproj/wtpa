Essential Spearfishing Gear for Key West Waters

✍️ Captain Dane Karcher 📅 April 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read

Spearfishing success depends on quality equipment. The right gear makes the difference between a frustrating day and an incredible experience. This guide breaks down every essential item, from beginner to advanced, with recommendations.

The Speargun: Your Primary Weapon

Beginners: Start with a pole spear. It's safer, easier to control, and perfect for reef hunting. Less penetrating power than a gun, but ideal for learning.

Intermediate: A band-powered speargun (rubber bands) offers moderate power and accuracy. Great for 30–50 foot dives.

Advanced: Pneumatic spearguns (air-powered) deliver maximum penetration and power. Requires training and experience.

Budget Speargun Option

Pole spear: $50–150 | Band gun: $200–400 | Pneumatic: $500+

Wetsuit Selection

Key West's warm water allows for lighter wetsuits than cold climates. A 3mm suit provides thermal protection and guards against coral scrapes and fish spines.

  • 3mm Wetsuit: Standard for Key West year-round. Quality 3mm suits run $100–250
  • 2mm Wetsuit: Lighter, for warm days (80°F+)
  • 5mm Wetsuit: Only needed in deep winter dives (rare in Key West)

Good brands: Cressi, Mares, O'Neill. Buy from a dive shop where you can try it on—fit is critical.

Fins: Power & Efficiency

Long-blade fins are essential for spearfishing. They provide power for deeper dives and efficiency at depth.

  • Blade Length: 20–26 inches (longer = more power, more effort)
  • Stiffness: Medium-stiff recommended (stiffer for deeper dives)
  • Material: Split fins reduce leg fatigue; paddle fins offer more power
  • Cost: $80–200 per pair

Quality fins from Mares, Scubapro, or Cressi last years and make a huge difference in comfort and performance.

Mask & Snorkel

📿 Mask Essentials

Get a low-volume diving mask (not snorkeling mask). Low-volume means less air space, less pressure squeeze at depth, and easier equalization. Cost: $40–80

Choose quality optics if you have vision needs. Prescription lenses available ($80–150).

Snorkel: Simple is best. A basic J-tube snorkel ($20–30) is all you need. Avoid fancy purge valves—they break.

Spear Shafts & Heads

The spear shaft and head (tip) matter tremendously. Different heads for different fish:

  • 3-prong/3-barb head: General purpose, good for most fish
  • Single barb: Cleaner penetration, less drag
  • Slip-tip head: Disconnects from shaft after shot; reduces weight and drag
  • Cost: Heads $15–40 each; shafts $30–80

Quality spear tips from reputable brands are worth the investment. Cheap tips bend and break.

Weighting & Buoyancy

Proper weighting is critical for depth control and efficiency. You'll need:

  • Weight belt: $30–60 (get one with quick-release for safety)
  • Individual weights: 2–5 lb lead pieces
  • Target weighting: Neutral at 10 feet, sinking at ~1 ft/sec

Get your weighting right in a pool before going to the ocean. Too light = can't reach depth. Too heavy = dangerous descent.

Dive Knife & Safety Gear

A quality dive knife ($30–80) is essential for cutting fishing line if tangled, clearing kelp, or self-defense. Get a fixed blade (not folding) for reliability.

Fish stringer: Keep your catch secure with a quality stringer. Prevents loss of hard-won fish. Cost: $10–30.

Safety Equipment

These are non-negotiable:

  • Dive flag & buoy: Red & white flag marks your location ($20–40). Required by law.
  • Dive light: Even daytime dives benefit from a small light for crevice hunting ($40–80)
  • Depth gauge: Track your dives and avoid pushing too deep ($20–50)
  • Watch or dive computer: Monitor dive duration ($50–300+)

Where to Buy

For best selection and fit: Visit a local dive shop. Quality shops fit you properly and offer warranty support.

For convenience: Amazon carries most spearfishing gear, but try local first for critical items like wetsuits and masks.

Maintenance Tips

  • Rinse gear in fresh water after every dive
  • Dry completely before storage
  • Store in cool, dark place (sunlight damages rubber)
  • Replace spear shafts if bent (don't reuse damaged equipment)
  • Service spearguns annually (have bands checked)
